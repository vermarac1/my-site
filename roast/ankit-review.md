# Ankit Verma — Growth Marketer Review

**Assignment:** 5-second test: do I understand the value prop? Would I convert? Where do visitors drop off?

---

## Verdict: Value prop passes the 5-second test. Conversion path fails the 5-minute test.

---

### The 5-Second Test

Lands on hero. Reads: "Your pipeline is not failing because of effort."

Immediate reaction: *yes that's me, keep reading.*

Sub-headline: "It is failing because the GTM system underneath it is leaking revenue. We diagnose where qualified demand breaks."

Clear enough. I understand what you do. The 5-second test passes.

---

### Where It Falls Apart

**The CTA is exhausted by section 3.**

I counted 9 separate calls to action on one page. Every section ends with "Book a diagnostic call." The first time, it's compelling. The third time, it feels like a toll booth. The seventh time, I'm ignoring it.

Conversion psychology: a CTA repeated too many times signals desperation. It also removes choice architecture — if everything is equally urgent, nothing is. The page should have one primary CTA (hero), one mid-page CTA (after the strongest proof point), and one final CTA. Three maximum on a page this length.

**The $200M+ stat is an active conversion risk.**

Hero stats: "15+ Years", "$200M+ Pipeline influenced", "50+ Global campaigns delivered", "4 Operating markets."

The case studies on the same page show: $2M+ revenue contribution, 45% lead improvement, 35% conversion improvement. There is no path from the case study numbers to $200M+ in pipeline. Any B2B buyer — the exact ICP this site is targeting — will notice this mismatch in seconds. They spend their working lives looking at pipeline numbers.

Either justify the $200M+ (with context like "across 15 years and 8 companies, total pipeline generated by GTM programmes we designed and operated") or replace it with the verifiable $2M+ number with a cleaner label.

**"Operators" as a nav label will confuse first-time visitors.**

The nav reads: Diagnosis / Engagements / Case Files / Operators / FAQ.

"Operators" means nothing to a B2B SaaS founder who just landed on this page. "Team" is the expected word. Using "Operators" is either a brand affectation or an error, and first-time visitors cannot tell which. Confusion at the nav level means people skip the team section entirely.

**No pricing anywhere, and the FAQ does not address it.**

Intentional decision, per strategy. Fine for now. But the FAQ section has 6 questions and none of them is "What does this cost?" or "How do I know which engagement I need?" — the two questions every single prospect has. These should be in the FAQ even with directional answers ("Engagements are structured around defined scope rather than hourly billing. The Diagnostic Call is the right place to discuss fit and investment.").

**The "Index 01 — Revenue Architecture" eyebrow is invisible noise.**

At the top of the hero: "Index 01 — Revenue Architecture / B2B SaaS · FinTech · NaaS · IT". This looks like a file reference number that was never meant to be public-facing. It contributes nothing to conversion intent and will confuse visitors who do not understand the editorial conceit.

**50+ global campaigns delivered — this stat is suspicious.**

If I'm a B2B SaaS CEO evaluating a boutique firm, "50+ global campaigns delivered" means nothing. What kind of campaigns? What results? The stat sounds like it was added to fill a stat box, not because it's the most compelling number available. A number without context in a credibility-critical section is worse than no number.

### Drop-Off Prediction (Scroll Depth)

| Depth | Behavior |
|---|---|
| Hero | ~60% read past the fold |
| Diagnosis section | ~40% still engaged |
| Framework grid | ~30% — this is where the editorial becomes text-heavy |
| Engagements | ~25% — the ones who were already leaning toward booking |
| Case files | ~15% — only the methodical buyers |
| Methodology | ~10% |
| Team | ~8% |
| FAQ | ~6% |
| Final CTA | ~5% |

The page earns attention with the hero and loses most of it in the Diagnosis and Framework sections because there is no visual relief — just dense text and numbered lists. A single visual proof element (a redacted pipeline chart, a before/after diagram, a process flow) between sections 2 and 4 would arrest the scroll.

---

### Conversion Recommendation

**Move one testimonial or a visible client signal** (even anonymised: "Used by SaaS companies in India, UK, and North America") immediately after the case files section. Right now there is nothing between the case studies and the methodology section that gives a prospect permission to stop evaluating and start trusting.
