# Shreya Iyer — Brand Strategist Review

**Assignment:** Is the copy distinctive? Does it sound like a real person or like AI? Would I remember this site?

---

## Verdict: Polished. Forgettable. Not yet a brand.

---

### What Works

The hero headline — "Your pipeline is not failing because of effort" — is genuinely good. It has a specific insight. It challenges something the reader believes. It does not start with "We help B2B companies." That alone puts it in the top 20% of consulting websites I review.

The case file aesthetic is interesting. Naming sections "Case File No. 01 / 03" creates a distinctive frame that most competitors would never think of. If the whole site committed to this language, it would be memorable.

The voice is consistently dry and authoritative. No exclamation marks. No "We're passionate about." That restraint is earned and right.

---

### What Fails

**It reads like a smart AI trained on McKinsey decks.**

Every sentence is technically correct. None of them sound like they were written by a human who has scars. "The diagnostic structure behind every ScaleBuz engagement" — no person who has lived this work talks that way. "Built through operator accountability" — same problem. These constructions are precise but sterile.

The copy describes a methodology repeatedly but never makes me *feel* the problem. There is one moment in the diagnosis section — "So most companies do the obvious thing: more channels, more spend, more headcount. The underlying system stays broken." — where you can almost hear a voice. Everything else around it is clinical.

**"Revenue Architecture" as a category is doing zero work.**

It appears in the nav, the hero eyebrow, the brand sub-label, the FAQ, the methodology section. Nobody searches for "revenue architecture." Nobody wakes up thinking "I need a revenue architect." The category name requires explanation every time it appears, which means it is not a category — it is a description wearing a category's clothes.

Compare: "We diagnose why your pipeline is broken and fix it" is clear. "Revenue Architecture" is not. You need a category name that *creates* the frame without requiring the frame to be explained.

**The hero eyebrow — "Index 01 — Revenue Architecture" — is pretentious without payoff.**

What index? Index of what? This reads like an internal filing system that accidentally got printed on the customer-facing document. The section markers ("The Diagnosis · 02", "File 01 of 04") carry this problem throughout. Structural numerology without narrative purpose.

**"ScaleBuz" looks like a mistake.**

The camel-case brand name — Scale + Buz — reads as either a misspelling of "buzz" or a startup that couldn't get the domain. Every time it appears in body copy next to normally-cased words, it creates a small moment of doubt. The brand name is doing active damage to the premium positioning.

**No human moment anywhere.**

Rachit has 15 years of building these systems inside companies where he had no safety net. That is a remarkable and specific differentiator. It appears once, in the bio section, buried under a layer of formal credentials. The site never uses first person. There is no "I have built this eight times" moment that makes me trust the person behind it. It reads as "we" throughout, but it is two people, and that makes the institutional voice feel like a performance.

---

### Would I Remember This Site?

For 48 hours, maybe. A week later, I might remember "the B2B pipeline consultancy with the interesting case file format." The copy, I would not remember. No phrase from this site would stay with me except the hero headline.

---

### One Thing That Must Change Before Launch

**Replace "Revenue Architecture" with a plain-language positioning line in the hero and nav.** Keep it as an internal category name if useful. But the first thing a cold visitor reads should not require them to understand a proprietary concept to understand what you do.
